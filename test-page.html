UX · Strategy

Asda Design Guidelines

As Lead Web Designer at Asda, I owned the end-to-end creative specification process for digital display media — then identified why suppliers kept getting it wrong, and redesigned the entire information experience around them.

The Problem

Suppliers were submitting non-compliant media at a significant rate — not because the rules didn't exist, but because the PDF guidelines weren't usable. The account team was absorbing the cost in rejections and back-and-forth.

My Role

Lead Web Designer — responsible for defining the creative specifications, designing the banner templates from scratch, and redesigning the entire guidelines experience based on supplier research.

The Problem

UX Research · Problem Framing

As Lead Web Designer, I was responsible for defining the creative specifications for Asda's full suite of digital display formats — designing the banner templates from scratch and setting the technical and accessibility requirements suppliers had to meet. The specs existed. The problem was that suppliers couldn't reliably act on them.

PDF-based guidelines were the only reference point, and they weren't built around how suppliers actually worked. The result was a high volume of rejected media submissions, repeated questions to account managers, and a process that was failing both the internal team and the suppliers trying to navigate it.

Process & Approach

Information Architecture · UI Design · Build

Rather than updating the PDFs, I redesigned the guidelines from the ground up as a bespoke interactive hub — structured around how suppliers browse and make decisions, not how the internal team had always organised the information. Each media format got its own dedicated page with in-situ examples, accessibility guidance, downloadable templates, and direct links to the relevant Photoshop UI toolkit.

The hub was aligned to Asda's brand guidelines throughout, and the architecture was later adapted for Asda's new CMS — maintaining the original structure while moving to standardised, modular components.

Usability Testing

Research · Synthesis · Iteration

Before launch I ran a moderated usability study with five suppliers — a mix of regular submitters and those with a history of media rejections. Using a think-aloud protocol, participants were tasked with locating specs for a specific format, checking the accessibility requirements, and downloading the correct template. I observed without intervening and affinity-mapped the findings afterwards.

Three themes emerged clearly, each pointing to a gap between how the guidelines were structured and how suppliers were actually trying to use them.

Finding 1 — Navigation wasn't scannable

Suppliers were hunting for specific formats rather than reading linearly, but the anchor links gave no indication they were navigational. Adding a "Jump to:" label resolved the hesitation immediately in follow-up testing.

Finding 2 — Accessibility requirements lacked context

Suppliers understood the rules but not the reasoning — so requirements felt arbitrary and were easier to dismiss. The underlying issue was that the same banner runs across multiple placements, meaning copy had to be legible at the smallest rendered size. To make this tangible, I built a Content Checker Tool that let suppliers preview exactly how their artwork would appear on site before submitting.

Finding 3 — The Photoshop UI toolkit wasn't self-explanatory

Suppliers had the right file but no clear entry point into it. File structure, layer naming, and export settings weren't obvious without guidance. A step-by-step video tutorial was added to the toolkit section to close that gap.

Results

Impact · Outcomes

Surfacing the right information in the right format — and closing the gaps usability testing exposed — had a measurable effect. Supplier questions to the account team dropped significantly, and the media implementation rate rose from 95% to 98%. At the volume Asda's campaigns team was handling, that's a meaningful shift.
